In a typical wiki, text is written using a simplified <a href="/wiki/Markup_language" title="Markup language">markup language</a> and often edited with the help of a <a href="/wiki/Online_rich-text_editor" title="Online rich-text editor">rich-text editor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A wiki is run using <a href="/wiki/Wiki_software" title="Wiki software">wiki software</a>, otherwise known as a wiki engine. A wiki engine is a type of <a href="/wiki/Content_management_system" title="Content management system">content management system</a>, but it differs from most other such systems, including <a href="/wiki/Blog_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Blog software">blog software</a>, in that the content is created without any defined owner or leader, and wikis have little inherent structure, allowing structure to emerge according to the needs of the users.<sup id="cite_ref-Easy_Wiki_Hosting_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Easy_Wiki_Hosting-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> There are dozens of different wiki engines in use, both standalone and part of other software, such as <a href="/wiki/Bug_tracking_system" title="Bug tracking system">bug tracking systems</a>. Some wiki engines are <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open source</a>, whereas others are <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a>. Some permit control over different functions (levels of access); for example, editing rights may permit changing, adding, or removing material. Others may permit access without enforcing access control. Other rules may be imposed to organize content.
</p><p>The online encyclopedia project <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> is the most popular wiki-based website, and is one of the most widely viewed sites in the world, having been ranked in the top ten since 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexa_Top_Sites_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexa_Top_Sites-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Wikipedia is not a single wiki but rather a collection of hundreds of wikis, with each one pertaining to a specific language. In addition to Wikipedia, there are hundreds of thousands of other wikis in use, both public and private, including wikis functioning as <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge management</a> resources, <a href="/wiki/Notetaking_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Notetaking software">notetaking</a> tools, <a href="/wiki/Web_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Web community">community websites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Intranet" title="Intranet">intranets</a>. The English-language Wikipedia has the largest collection of articles: as of February 2020, it has over 6 million articles.  <a href="/wiki/Ward_Cunningham" title="Ward Cunningham">Ward Cunningham</a>, the developer of the first wiki software, <a href="/wiki/WikiWikiWeb" title="WikiWikiWeb">WikiWikiWeb</a>, originally described wiki as "the simplest online database that could possibly work".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wiki#Hawaiian" class="extiw" title="wikt:wiki">Wiki</a>" (pronounced <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Hawaiian" title="Help:IPA/Hawaiian">[ˈwiki]</a></span><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">Hawaiian</a> word meaning "quick".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Ward Cunningham and co-author <a href="/wiki/Bo_Leuf" title="Bo Leuf">Bo Leuf</a>, in their book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wiki_Way" title="The Wiki Way">The Wiki Way: Quick Collaboration on the Web</a></i>, described the essence of the Wiki concept as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A wiki invites all users—not just experts—to edit any <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">page</a> or to create new pages within the wiki Web site, using only a standard <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_software" title="Vanilla software">"plain-vanilla"</a> Web browser without any extra <a href="/wiki/Browser_extension" title="Browser extension">add-ons</a>.</li>
<li>Wiki promotes meaningful topic associations between different pages by making page link creation intuitively easy and showing whether an intended target page exists or not.</li>
<li>A wiki is <i>not</i> a carefully crafted site created by experts and professional writers, and designed for casual visitors. Instead, it seeks to involve the typical visitor/user in an ongoing process of creation and collaboration that constantly changes the website landscape.</li></ul>
<p>A wiki enables communities of editors and contributors to write documents collaboratively. All that people require to contribute is a computer, <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> access, a <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a>, and a basic understanding of a simple <a href="/wiki/Markup_language" title="Markup language">markup language</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a>). A single page in a wiki website is referred to as a "wiki page", while the entire collection of pages, which are usually well-interconnected by <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlinks</a>, is "the wiki". A wiki is essentially a database for creating, browsing, and searching through information. A wiki allows non-linear, evolving, complex, and networked text, while also allowing for editor argument, debate, and interaction regarding the content and formatting.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A defining characteristic of wiki technology is the ease with which pages can be created and updated. Generally, there is no review by a moderator or gatekeeper before modifications are accepted and thus lead to changes on the website. Many wikis are open to alteration by the general public without requiring registration of <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">user</a> accounts. Many edits can be made in <a href="/wiki/Real-time_web" title="Real-time web">real-time</a> and appear almost instantly online, but this feature facilitates abuse of the system. Private wiki servers require <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">user authentication</a> to edit pages, and sometimes even to read them. <a href="/wiki/Maged_N._Kamel_Boulos" title="Maged N. Kamel Boulos">Maged N. Kamel Boulos</a>, Cito Maramba, and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wheeler" title="Steve Wheeler">Steve Wheeler</a> write that the open wikis produce a process of <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">Social Darwinism</a>. <span style="padding-right:.15em;">"</span>'Unfit' sentences and sections are ruthlessly culled, edited, and replaced if they are not considered 'fit', which hopefully results in the evolution of a higher quality and more relevant page. While such <a href="/wiki/Openness" title="Openness">openness</a> may invite 'vandalism' and the posting of untrue information, this same openness also makes it possible to rapidly correct or restore a 'quality' wiki page."<sup id="cite_ref-BMC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMC-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation">Navigation</span></h4>
<p>Some wikis have an Edit button or link directly on the page being viewed, if the user has permission to edit the page. This can lead to a text-based editing page where participants can structure and format wiki pages with a simplified markup language, sometimes known as <b>Wikitext</b>, <b>Wiki markup</b> or <b>Wikicode</b> (it can also lead to a <a href="/wiki/WYSIWYG" title="WYSIWYG">WYSIWYG</a> editing page; see <a href="#Visual_editing">the paragraph after the table below</a>). For example, starting lines of text with <a href="/wiki/Asterisk" title="Asterisk">asterisks</a> could create a <a href="/wiki/Bullet_(typography)" title="Bullet (typography)">bulleted list</a>. The style and syntax of wikitexts can vary greatly among wiki implementations,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE"><span title="An editor has requested that an example be provided. (August 2018)">example  needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> some of which also allow <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> tags.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Consistency">Consistency</span></h4>
<p>Wikis have favoured plain-text editing, with fewer and simpler conventions than HTML, for indicating style and structure. Although limiting access to HTML and <a href="/wiki/Cascading_Style_Sheets" title="Cascading Style Sheets">Cascading Style Sheets</a> (CSS) of wikis limits user ability to alter the structure and formatting of wiki content, there are some benefits. Limited access to CSS promotes consistency in the look and feel, and having <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> disabled prevents a user from implementing code that may limit other users' access.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_editing">Visual editing</span></h4>
<p>Wikis can also make <a href="/wiki/WYSIWYG" title="WYSIWYG">WYSIWYG</a> editing available to users, usually by means of JavaScript control that translates graphically entered formatting instructions into the corresponding <a href="/wiki/HTML_element" title="HTML element">HTML tags</a> or wikitext. In those implementations, the markup of a newly edited, marked-up version of the page is generated and submitted to the server <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(human-computer_interaction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Transparency (human-computer interaction)">transparently</a>, shielding the user from this technical detail. An example of this is the <a href="/wiki/VisualEditor" title="VisualEditor">VisualEditor</a> on Wikipedia. WYSIWYG controls do not, however, always provide all of the features available in wikitext, and some users prefer not to use a WYSIWYG editor. Hence, many of these sites offer some means to edit the wikitext directly.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Version_history">Version history</span></h4>
<p>Some wikis keep a record of changes made to wiki pages; often, every version of the page is stored. This means that authors can revert to an older version of the page should it be necessary because a mistake has been made, such as the content accidentally being deleted or the page has been vandalized to include offensive or malicious text or other inappropriate content.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Edit_summary">Edit summary</span></h4>
<p>Many wiki implementations, such as <a href="/wiki/MediaWiki" title="MediaWiki">MediaWiki</a>, the software that powers <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, allow users to supply an <i>edit summary</i> when they edit a page. This is a short piece of text summarizing the changes they have made (e.g., "Corrected grammar," or "Fixed formatting in table."). It is not inserted into the article's main text, but is stored along with that revision of the page, allowing users to explain what has been done and why, similar to a log message when making changes in a <a href="/wiki/Revision_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Revision control">revision-control</a> system. This enables other users to see which changes have been made by whom and why, often in a list of summaries, dates and other short, relevant content, a list which is called a "log" or "history."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation_2">Navigation</span></h3>
<p>Within the text of most pages, there are usually many <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">hypertext</a> links to other pages within the wiki. This form of non-linear navigation is more "native" to a wiki than structured/formalized navigation schemes. Users can also create any number of index or table-of-contents pages, with hierarchical categorization or whatever form of organization they like. These may be challenging to maintain "by hand", as multiple authors and users may create and delete pages in an <a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a>, unorganized manner. Wikis can provide one or more ways to categorize or tag pages to support the maintenance of such index pages. Some wikis, including the original, have a <a href="/wiki/Backlink" title="Backlink">backlink</a> feature, which displays all pages that link to a given page. It is also typically possible in a wiki to create links to pages that do not yet exist, as a way to invite others to share what they know about a subject new to the wiki. Wiki users can typically "tag" pages with categories or keywords, to make it easier for other users to find the article. For example, a user creating a new article on <a href="/wiki/Cold_weather_cycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold weather cycling">cold weather cycling</a> might "tag" this page under the categories of commuting, winter sports and bicycling. This would make it easier for other users to find the article.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linking_and_creating_pages">Linking and creating pages</span></h3>
<p>Links are created using a specific syntax, the so-called "link pattern". Originally, most wikis<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> used <a href="/wiki/CamelCase" class="mw-redirect" title="CamelCase">CamelCase</a> to name pages and create links. These are produced by capitalizing words in a phrase and removing the spaces between them (the word "CamelCase" is itself an example). While CamelCase makes linking easy, it also leads to links in a form that deviates from the standard spelling. To link to a page with a single-word title, one must abnormally capitalize one of the letters in the word (e.g. "WiKi" instead of "Wiki"). CamelCase-based wikis are instantly recognizable because they have many links with names such as "TableOfContents" and "BeginnerQuestions." It is possible for a wiki to render the visible anchor of such links "pretty" by reinserting spaces, and possibly also reverting to lower case. This reprocessing of the link to improve the readability of the anchor is, however, limited by the loss of capitalization information caused by CamelCase reversal. For example, "RichardWagner" should be rendered as "Richard Wagner", whereas "PopularMusic" should be rendered as "popular music". There is no easy way to determine which <a href="/wiki/Capital_letter" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital letter">capital letters</a> should remain capitalized. As a result, many wikis now have "free linking" using brackets, and some disable CamelCase by default.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Searching">Searching</span></h3>
<p>Most wikis offer at least a title <a href="/wiki/Search_algorithm" title="Search algorithm">search</a>, and sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Full_text_search" class="mw-redirect" title="Full text search">full-text search</a>. The scalability of the search depends on whether the wiki engine uses a database. Some wikis, such as <a href="/wiki/PmWiki" title="PmWiki">PmWiki</a>, use <a href="/wiki/Flat_file" class="mw-redirect" title="Flat file">flat files</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Augar_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Augar-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> MediaWiki's first versions used flat files, but it was rewritten by <a href="/wiki/Lee_Daniel_Crocker" title="Lee Daniel Crocker">Lee Daniel Crocker</a> in the early 2000s (decade) to be a database application. Indexed database access is necessary for high speed searches on large wikis. Alternatively, external <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">search engines</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google Search</a> can sometimes be used on wikis with limited searching functions in order to obtain more precise results.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_wikis" title="History of wikis">History of wikis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg/220px-HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg/330px-HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg/440px-HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HNL_Wiki_Wiki_Bus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wiki_Wiki_Shuttle" title="Wiki Wiki Shuttle">Wiki Wiki Shuttle</a> at <a href="/wiki/Honolulu_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Honolulu International Airport">Honolulu International Airport</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/WikiWikiWeb" title="WikiWikiWeb">WikiWikiWeb</a> was the first wiki.<sup id="cite_ref-ebersbach10_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebersbach10-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Ward Cunningham started developing WikiWikiWeb in Portland, Oregon, in 1994, and installed it on the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">Internet domain</a> <a href="/wiki/C2.com" class="mw-redirect" title="C2.com">c2.com</a> on March 25, 1995. It was named by Cunningham, who remembered a <a href="/wiki/Honolulu_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Honolulu International Airport">Honolulu International Airport</a> counter employee telling him to take the "<a href="/wiki/Wiki_Wiki_Shuttle" title="Wiki Wiki Shuttle">Wiki Wiki Shuttle</a>" bus that runs between the airport's terminals. According to Cunningham, "I chose wiki-wiki as an alliterative substitute for 'quick' and thereby avoided naming this stuff quick-web."<sup id="cite_ref-cunningham_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cunningham-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-history_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cunningham was, in part, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>'s <a href="/wiki/HyperCard" title="HyperCard">HyperCard</a>, which he had used. HyperCard, however, was single-user.<sup id="cite_ref-artima_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-artima-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Apple had designed a system allowing users to create virtual "card stacks" supporting links among the various cards. Cunningham developed <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a>'s ideas by allowing users to "comment on and change one another's text."<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hypercard_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hypercard-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Cunningham says his goals were to link together people's experiences to create a new literature to document programming <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">patterns</a>, and to harness people's natural desire to talk and tell stories with a technology that would feel comfortable to those not used to "authoring".<sup id="cite_ref-artima_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-artima-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> became the most famous wiki site, launched in January 2001 and entering the top ten most popular websites in 2007. In the early 2000s (decade), wikis were increasingly adopted in enterprise as collaborative software. Common uses included project communication, intranets, and documentation, initially for technical users. Some <a href="/wiki/Corporate_wiki" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporate wiki">companies use wikis</a> as their only collaborative software and as a replacement for static intranets, and some schools and universities use wikis to enhance <a href="/wiki/Group_learning" class="mw-redirect" title="Group learning">group learning</a>. There may be greater use of wikis behind <a href="/wiki/Firewall_(computing)" title="Firewall (computing)">firewalls</a> than on the public Internet. On March 15, 2007, the word <i>wiki</i> was listed in the online <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED1-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_definitions">Alternative definitions</span></h2>
<p>In the late 1990s and early 2000s, the word "wiki" was used to refer to both user-editable websites and the software that powers them; the latter definition is still occasionally in use.<sup id="cite_ref-Easy_Wiki_Hosting_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Easy_Wiki_Hosting-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Wiki inventor Ward Cunningham wrote in 2014<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> that the word "wiki" should not be used to refer to a single website, but rather to a mass of user-editable pages and or sites, so that a single website is not "a wiki" but "an instance of wiki". He wrote that the concept of wiki federation, in which the same content can be hosted and edited in more than one location in a manner similar to <a href="/wiki/Distributed_version_control" title="Distributed version control">distributed version control</a>, meant that the concept of a single discrete "wiki" no longer made sense.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementations">Implementations</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_wiki_software" title="List of wiki software">List of wiki software</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wiki_software" title="Wiki software">Wiki software</a> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_software" title="Collaborative software">collaborative software</a> that runs a wiki system, allowing web pages to be created and edited using a common web browser. It may be implemented as a series of scripts behind an existing <a href="/wiki/Web_server" title="Web server">web server</a>, or as a standalone <a href="/wiki/Application_server" title="Application server">application server</a> that runs on one or more web servers. The content is stored in a <a href="/wiki/File_system" title="File system">file system</a>, and changes to the content are stored in a <a href="/wiki/Relational_database_management_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Relational database management system">relational database management system</a>. A commonly implemented software package is <a href="/wiki/MediaWiki" title="MediaWiki">MediaWiki</a>, which runs <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>. Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Personal_wiki" title="Personal wiki">personal wikis</a> run as a standalone application on a single computer. <a href="/wiki/WikidPad" title="WikidPad">WikidPad</a> is an example. One application, <a href="/wiki/TiddlyWiki" title="TiddlyWiki">TiddlyWiki</a>, simply makes use of an even single local HTML file with JavaScript inside.
</p><p>Wikis can also be created on a "<a href="/wiki/Wiki_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Wiki farm">wiki farm</a>", where the server-side software is implemented by the wiki farm owner. <a href="/wiki/PBwiki" class="mw-redirect" title="PBwiki">PBwiki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialtext" title="Socialtext">Socialtext</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wikia" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikia">Wikia</a> are popular examples of such services. Some wiki farms can also make private, password-protected wikis. Free wiki farms generally contain advertising on every page. For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_wiki_farms" class="mw-redirect" title="Comparison of wiki farms">Comparison of wiki farms</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trust_and_security">Trust and security</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controlling_changes">Controlling changes</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable"><div style="display:inline;" class="plainlinks selfreference noprint">"Recent changes" redirects here. For the Wikipedia help page, see <a href="/wiki/Help:Recent_changes" title="Help:Recent changes">Help:Recent changes</a>. For the recent changes page itself, see <a href="/wiki/Special:RecentChanges" title="Special:RecentChanges">Special:RecentChanges</a>.</div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:History_Comparison_Example_(Vector).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/History_Comparison_Example_%28Vector%29.png/220px-History_Comparison_Example_%28Vector%29.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/History_Comparison_Example_%28Vector%29.png/330px-History_Comparison_Example_%28Vector%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/History_Comparison_Example_%28Vector%29.png/440px-History_Comparison_Example_%28Vector%29.png 2x" data-file-width="1225" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:History_Comparison_Example_(Vector).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>History comparison reports highlight the changes between two revisions of a page.</div></div></div>
<p>Wikis are generally designed with the philosophy of making it easy to correct mistakes, rather than making it difficult to make them. Thus, while wikis are very open, they provide a means to verify the validity of recent additions to the body of pages. The most prominent, on almost every wiki, is the "Recent Changes" page—a specific list numbering recent edits, or a list of edits made within a given time frame.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Some wikis can filter the list to remove minor edits and edits made by automatic importing scripts ("<a href="/wiki/Internet_bot" title="Internet bot">bots</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> From the change log, other functions are accessible in most wikis: the <a href="/wiki/Changelog" title="Changelog">revision history</a> shows previous page versions and the <a href="/wiki/Diff" title="Diff">diff</a> feature highlights the changes between two revisions. Using the revision history, an editor can view and restore a previous version of the article. This gives great power to the author to eliminate edits. The diff feature can be used to decide whether or not this is necessary. A regular wiki user can view the diff of an edit listed on the "Recent Changes" page and, if it is an unacceptable edit, consult the history, restoring a previous revision; this process is more or less streamlined, depending on the wiki software used.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In case unacceptable edits are missed on the "recent changes" page, some wiki engines provide additional content control. It can be monitored to ensure that a page, or a set of pages, keeps its quality. A person willing to maintain pages will be warned of modifications to the pages, allowing them to verify the validity of new editions quickly. This can be seen as a very pro-author and anti-editor feature.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> A watchlist is a common implementation of this. Some wikis also implement "patrolled revisions", in which editors with the requisite credentials can mark some edits as not vandalism. A "flagged revisions" system can prevent edits from going live until they have been reviewed.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trustworthiness_and_reliability_of_content">Trustworthiness and reliability of content</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki-quality-dimensions.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Wiki-quality-dimensions.png/220px-Wiki-quality-dimensions.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Wiki-quality-dimensions.png/330px-Wiki-quality-dimensions.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Wiki-quality-dimensions.png/440px-Wiki-quality-dimensions.png 2x" data-file-width="1177" data-file-height="854" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki-quality-dimensions.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Quality dimensions of the wiki and other sources: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> use case<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div><p> Critics of publicly editable wiki systems argue that these systems could be easily tampered with by malicious individuals ("vandals") or even by well-meaning but unskilled users who introduce errors into the content. While proponents argue that the community of users can catch malicious content and correct it.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lars_Aronsson" title="Lars Aronsson">Lars Aronsson</a>, a data systems specialist, summarizes the controversy as follows: "Most people, when they first learn about the wiki concept, assume that a Web site that can be edited by anybody would soon be rendered useless by destructive input. It sounds like offering free spray cans next to a grey concrete wall. The only likely outcome would be ugly <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> and simple tagging, and many artistic efforts would not be long lived. Still, it seems to work very well."<sup id="cite_ref-ebersbach10_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebersbach10-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> High editorial standards in medicine and health sciences articles, in which users typically use peer-reviewed journals or university textbooks as sources, have led to the idea of expert-moderated wikis.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Some wikis allow one to link to specific versions of articles, which has been useful to the scientific community, in that expert peer reviewers could analyse articles, improve them and provide links to the trusted version of that article.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Noveck points out that "participants are accredited by members of the wiki community, who have a vested interest in preserving the quality of the work product, on the basis of their ongoing participation." On controversial topics that have been subject to disruptive editing, a wiki author may restrict editing to registered users.<sup id="cite_ref-Noveck_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noveck-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Security">Security</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Edit war" redirects here. For Wikipedia's policy on edit warring, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Edit_warring" title="Wikipedia:Edit warring">Wikipedia:Edit warring</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Edit war" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Edit_conflict" title="Edit conflict">Edit conflict</a>.</div>
<p>The open philosophy of wiki&#160;– allowing anyone to edit content – does not ensure that every editor's intentions are well-mannered. For example, <a href="/wiki/Cybervandalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Cybervandalism">vandalism</a> (changing wiki content to something offensive, adding nonsense, or deliberately adding incorrect information, such as <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a> information) can be a major problem. On larger wiki sites, such as those run by the <a href="/wiki/Wikimedia_Foundation" title="Wikimedia Foundation">Wikimedia Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a> <i>can </i>go unnoticed for some period of time. Wikis, because of their open nature, are susceptible to intentional disruption, known as "<a href="/wiki/Troll_(Internet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Troll (Internet)">trolling</a>".
Wikis tend to take a <i><a href="/wiki/Soft_security" title="Soft security">soft-security</a></i> approach to the problem of vandalism, making damage easy to undo rather than attempting to prevent damage. Larger wikis often employ sophisticated methods, such as bots that automatically identify and revert vandalism and JavaScript enhancements that show characters that have been added in each edit. In this way, vandalism can be limited to just "minor vandalism" or "sneaky vandalism", where the characters added/eliminated are so few that bots do not identify them and users do not pay much attention to them.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag relies on an unreliable source. (July 2013)">unreliable source</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> An example of a bot that reverts vandalism on Wikipedia is ClueBot NG. ClueBot NG can revert edits, often within minutes, if not seconds. The bot uses <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a> in lieu of <a href="/wiki/Heuristic_(computer_science)" title="Heuristic (computer science)">heuristics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The amount of vandalism a wiki receives depends on how open the wiki is. For instance, some wikis allow unregistered users, identified by their <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a>, to edit content, while others limit this function to just registered users. Most wikis allow anonymous editing without an account,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> but give registered users additional editing functions; on most wikis, becoming a registered user is a short and simple process. Some wikis require an additional waiting period before gaining access to certain tools. For example, on the <a href="/wiki/English_Wikipedia" title="English Wikipedia">English Wikipedia</a>, registered users can rename pages only if their account is at least four days old and has made at least ten edits. Other wikis such as the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Wikipedia" title="Portuguese Wikipedia">Portuguese Wikipedia</a> use an editing requirement instead of a time requirement, granting extra tools after the user has made a certain number of edits to prove their trustworthiness and usefulness as an editor. <a href="/wiki/Vandalism_of_Wikipedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Vandalism of Wikipedia">Vandalism of Wikipedia</a> is common (though policed and usually reverted) because it is extremely open, allowing anyone with a computer and Internet access to edit it, although this makes it grow rapidly. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Citizendium" title="Citizendium">Citizendium</a> requires an editor's real name and short autobiography, affecting the growth of the wiki but sometimes helping stop vandalism.
</p><p>Edit wars can also occur as users repetitively revert a page to the version they favor. In some cases, editors with opposing views of which content should appear or what formatting style should be used will change and re-change each other's edits. This results in the page being "unstable" from a general users' perspective, because each time a general user comes to the page, it may look different. Some wiki software allows an administrator to stop such edit wars by locking a page from further editing until a decision has been made on what version of the page would be most appropriate.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Some wikis are in a better position than others to control behavior due to governance structures existing outside the wiki. For instance, a college teacher can create incentives for students to behave themselves on a class wiki they administer by limiting editing to logged-in users and pointing out that all contributions can be traced back to the contributors. Bad behavior can then be dealt with in accordance with university policies.<sup id="cite_ref-Augar_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Augar-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The issue of wiki vandalism is debated. In some cases, when an editor deletes an entire article and replaces it with nonsense content, it may be a "test edit", made by the user as she or he is experimenting with the wiki system. Some editors may not realize that they have damaged the page, or if they do realize it, they may not know how to undo the mistake or restore the content.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_malware_vector">Potential malware vector</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">Malware</a> can also be a problem for wikis, as users can add links to sites hosting malicious code. For example, a German Wikipedia article about the <a href="/wiki/Blaster_Worm" class="mw-redirect" title="Blaster Worm">Blaster Worm</a> was edited to include a hyperlink to a malicious website. Users of vulnerable Microsoft Windows systems who followed the link would be infected.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A countermeasure is the use of software that prevents users from saving an edit that contains a link to a site listed on a <a href="/wiki/Blacklist" class="mw-redirect" title="Blacklist">blacklist</a> of malicious sites.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communities">Communities</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8e/EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png/220px-EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8e/EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png/330px-EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8e/EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png/440px-EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png 2x" data-file-width="1258" data-file-height="854" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EnglishWikipedia_29June2017.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The home page of the English Wikipedia</div></div></div>
<p>The English Wikipedia has the largest user base among wikis on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and ranks in the top 10 among all Web sites in terms of traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Other large wikis include the <a href="/wiki/WikiWikiWeb" title="WikiWikiWeb">WikiWikiWeb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memory_Alpha" title="Memory Alpha">Memory Alpha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wikivoyage" title="Wikivoyage">Wikivoyage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Susning.nu" title="Susning.nu">Susning.nu</a>, a Swedish-language knowledge base. <a href="/wiki/List_of_medical_wikis" title="List of medical wikis">Medical</a> and health-related wiki examples include <a href="/wiki/Ganfyd" title="Ganfyd">Ganfyd</a>, an online collaborative medical reference that is edited by medical professionals and invited non-medical experts.<sup id="cite_ref-BMC_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMC-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Many wiki <a href="/wiki/Online_community" title="Online community">communities</a> are private, particularly within <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_software" title="Enterprise software">enterprises</a>. They are often used as <a href="/wiki/Internal_documentation" title="Internal documentation">internal documentation</a> for in-house systems and applications. Some companies use wikis to allow customers to help produce software documentation.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> A study of corporate wiki users found that they could be divided into "synthesizers" and "adders" of content. Synthesizers' frequency of contribution was affected more by their impact on other wiki users, while adders' contribution frequency was affected more by being able to accomplish their immediate work.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> from a study of 1000s of wiki deployments, Jonathan Grudin concluded careful stakeholder analysis and education are crucial to successful wiki deployment.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, the Gartner Group, noting the increasing popularity of wikis, estimated that they would become mainstream collaboration tools in at least 50% of companies by 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (July 2013)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Wikis can be used for <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag relies on an unreliable source. (July 2013)">unreliable source</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Wikis have also been used in the academic community for sharing and dissemination of information across institutional and international boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In those settings, they have been found useful for collaboration on <a href="/wiki/Grant_writing" title="Grant writing">grant writing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strategic_planning" title="Strategic planning">strategic planning</a>, departmental documentation, and committee work.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In the mid-2000s (decade), the increasing trend among industries toward collaboration was placing a heavier impetus upon educators to make students proficient in collaborative work, inspiring even greater interest in wikis being used in the classroom.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wikis have found some use within the legal profession, and within government. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Intellipedia" title="Intellipedia">Intellipedia</a>, designed to share and collect <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_assessment" title="Intelligence assessment">intelligence</a>, dKospedia, which was used by the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> to assist with review of documents pertaining to internment of detainees in <a href="/wiki/Guant%C3%A1namo_Bay" title="Guantánamo Bay">Guantánamo Bay</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and the wiki of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Seventh_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit</a>, used to post court rules and allow practitioners to comment and ask questions. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">United States Patent and Trademark Office</a> operates <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-Patent" title="Peer-to-Patent">Peer-to-Patent</a>, a wiki to allow the public to collaborate on finding <a href="/wiki/Prior_art" title="Prior art">prior art</a> relevant to examination of pending patent applications. <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>, New York has used a wiki to allow citizens to collaborate on the design and planning of a local park. <a href="/wiki/Cornell_Law_School" title="Cornell Law School">Cornell Law School</a> founded a wiki-based legal dictionary called Wex, whose growth has been hampered by restrictions on who can edit.<sup id="cite_ref-Noveck_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noveck-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In academic context, wiki has also been used as project collaboration and research support system.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_wikis">City wikis</span></h3>
<p>A city wiki (or local wiki) is a wiki used as a <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge base</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social network</a> for a specific <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> locale.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The term 'city wiki' or its foreign language equivalent (e.g. German 'Stadtwiki') is sometimes also used for wikis that cover not just a city, but a small town or an entire region. A city wiki contains information about specific instances of things, ideas, people and places. Much of this information might not be appropriate for <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia" title="Encyclopedia">encyclopedias</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> (e.g., articles on every retail outlet in a town), but might be appropriate for a wiki with more localized content and viewers. A city wiki could also contain information about the following subjects, that may or may not be appropriate for a general knowledge wiki, such as:
</p>
<ul><li>Details of public establishments such as public houses, bars, accommodation or social centers</li>
<li>Owner name, opening hours and statistics for a specific shop</li>
<li>Statistical information about a specific road in a city</li>
<li>Flavors of ice cream served at a local ice cream parlor</li>
<li>A biography of a local mayor and other persons</li></ul>

<p>WikiNodes are pages on wikis that describe related wikis. They are usually organized as neighbors and delegates. A <i>neighbor</i> wiki is simply a wiki that may discuss similar content or may otherwise be of interest. A <i>delegate</i> wiki is a wiki that agrees to have certain content delegated to that wiki.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> One way of finding a wiki on a specific subject is to follow the wiki-node network from wiki to wiki; another is to take a Wiki "bus tour", for example: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:TourBusStop" title="Wikipedia:TourBusStop">Wikipedia's Tour Bus Stop</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participants">Participants</span></h3>
<p>The four basic types of users who participate in wikis are reader, author, wiki administrator and system administrator. The system administrator is responsible for installation and maintenance of the wiki engine and the container web server. The wiki administrator maintains wiki content and is provided additional functions pertaining to pages (e.g. page protection and deletion), and can adjust users' access rights by, for instance, blocking them from editing.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_factors">Growth factors</span></h3>
<p>A study of several hundred wikis showed that a relatively high number of administrators for a given content size is likely to reduce growth;<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> that access controls restricting editing to registered users tends to reduce growth; that a lack of such access controls tends to fuel new user registration; and that higher administration ratios (i.e. admins/user) have no significant effect on content or population growth.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conferences">Conferences</span></h2>
<p>Active conferences and meetings about wiki-related topics include:
</p>
<ul><li>Atlassian Summit, an annual conference for users of <a href="/wiki/Atlassian" title="Atlassian">Atlassian</a> software, including <a href="/wiki/Confluence_(software)" title="Confluence (software)">Confluence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/OpenSym" title="OpenSym">OpenSym</a> (called WikiSym until 2014), an <a href="/wiki/Academic_conference" title="Academic conference">academic conference</a> dedicated to research about wikis and open collaboration.</li>
<li>SMWCon, a bi-annual conference for users and developers of <a href="/wiki/Semantic_MediaWiki" title="Semantic MediaWiki">Semantic MediaWiki</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>TikiFest, a frequently held meeting for users and developers of <a href="/wiki/Tiki_Wiki_CMS_Groupware" title="Tiki Wiki CMS Groupware">Tiki Wiki CMS Groupware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wikimania" title="Wikimania">Wikimania</a>, an annual conference dedicated to the research and practice of <a href="/wiki/Wikimedia_Foundation" title="Wikimedia Foundation">Wikimedia Foundation</a> projects like Wikipedia.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span></h2>
<p>Wikis typically have a set of rules governing user behavior. Wikipedia, for instance, has an extensive set of policies and guidelines summed up in its five pillars: Wikipedia is an encyclopedia; Wikipedia has a neutral point of view; Wikipedia is free content; Wikipedians should interact in a respectful and civil manner; and Wikipedia does not have firm rules. Many wikis have adopted a set of commandments. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Conservapedia" title="Conservapedia">Conservapedia</a> commands, among other things, that its editors use "<a href="/wiki/Before_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Before Christ">B.C.</a>" rather than "<a href="/wiki/B.C.E." class="mw-redirect" title="B.C.E.">B.C.E.</a>" when referring to years prior to <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">C.E.</a> 1 and refrain from "unproductive activity."<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> One teacher instituted a commandment for a class wiki, "<a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="The Golden Rule">Wiki unto others as you would have them wiki unto you</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Augar_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Augar-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_environment">Legal environment</span></h2>
<p>Joint authorship of articles, in which different users participate in correcting, editing, and compiling the finished product, can also cause editors to become <a href="/wiki/Tenants_in_common" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenants in common">tenants in common</a> of the copyright, making it impossible to republish without permission of all co-owners, some of whose identities may be unknown due to pseudonymous or anonymous editing.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Where persons contribute to a <a href="/wiki/Collective_work" title="Collective work">collective work</a> such as an encyclopedia, there is, however, no joint ownership if the contributions are separate and distinguishable.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Despite most wikis' tracking of individual contributions, the action of contributing to a wiki page is still arguably one of jointly correcting, editing, or compiling, which would give rise to joint ownership. Some copyright issues can be alleviated through the use of an <a href="/wiki/Open_content" title="Open content">open content</a> license. Version 2 of the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Free_Documentation_License" title="GNU Free Documentation License">GNU Free Documentation License</a> includes a specific provision for wiki relicensing; <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons" title="Creative Commons">Creative Commons</a> licenses are also popular. When no license is specified, an implied license to read and add content to a wiki may be deemed to exist on the grounds of business necessity and the inherent nature of a wiki, although the legal basis for such an implied license may not exist in all circumstances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Wikis and their users can be held liable for certain activities that occur on the wiki. If a wiki owner displays indifference and forgoes controls (such as banning copyright infringers) that he could have exercised to stop copyright infringement, he may be deemed to have authorized infringement, especially if the wiki is primarily used to infringe copyrights or obtains direct financial benefit, such as advertising revenue, from infringing activities.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, wikis may benefit from <a href="/wiki/Section_230_of_the_Communications_Decency_Act" title="Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act">Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act</a>, which protects sites that engage in "<a href="/wiki/Good_Samaritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Good Samaritan">Good Samaritan</a>" policing of harmful material, with no requirement on the quality or quantity of such self-policing.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> It has also been argued, however, that a wiki's enforcement of certain rules, such as anti-bias, verifiability, reliable sourcing, and no-original-research policies, could pose legal risks.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a> occurs on a wiki, theoretically all users of the wiki can be held liable, because any of them had the ability to remove or amend the defamatory material from the "publication." It remains to be seen whether wikis will be regarded as more akin to an <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">internet service provider</a>, which is generally not held liable due to its lack of control over publications' contents, than a publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-Legal_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legal-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It has been recommended that trademark owners monitor what information is presented about their trademarks on wikis, since courts may use such content as evidence pertaining to public perceptions. Joshua Jarvis notes, "Once misinformation is identified, the trade mark owner can simply edit the entry."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
